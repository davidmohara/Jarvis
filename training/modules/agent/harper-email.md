## What You'll Learn

How to draft emails through Harper that are calibrated for recipient, relationship, context, and purpose. Not just writing — strategic communication.

## Before We Start

No hard prerequisites. This module can be done anytime.

## Walkthrough

### Step 1: Trigger an Email Draft (2 min)

Say "draft an email to [person] about [topic]" or trigger the `harper-email` skill.

Harper asks:
- Who's the recipient?
- What's the purpose? (Request, update, follow-up, introduction, bad news)
- What tone? (Harper infers from relationship but asks for confirmation)

### Step 2: Context Calibration (3 min)

Harper pulls context:
- Relationship with the recipient (internal/external, seniority, past interactions)
- Topic history (previous emails, meetings, or notes on this subject)
- Organizational context (their role, your role, the dynamic)

**Coaching prompt:** "The same message reads completely differently depending on who receives it. 'Let's connect this week' from a CEO to a vendor is a command. From a VP to a peer, it's a request. Harper calibrates for this."

Walk through how the tone adjusts:
- Email to a direct report: warm, direct, actionable
- Email to a client: professional, value-focused, respectful of their time
- Email to a board member: concise, data-driven, headline first
- Email to a peer: collaborative, casual but clear

### Step 3: Draft and Iterate (3 min)

Harper produces the first draft. Review with the user:

- "Does this sound like you?"
- "Is the ask clear?"
- "Is the tone right for this relationship?"

Iterate if needed. Most emails need 1-2 rounds.

**Coaching prompt for each role:**
- **CEO**: "Shorter is better. Lead with the headline. If they need detail, they'll ask."
- **VP**: "Balance warmth with clarity. Don't bury the ask in paragraph three."
- **COS**: "You're often writing on behalf of someone else. Make sure the voice matches."

### Step 4: The 2-Minute Rule (2 min)

**Coaching prompt:** "If an email takes more than 2 minutes to read, it's a document — not an email. Harper's job is to keep it tight. If you find yourself wanting to add more, ask: should this be a meeting instead?"

Show the user the word count and estimated read time. If it's over 200 words, challenge: "Can we cut this in half?"

## Reflection

Ask: "Is there an email you've been putting off writing? What's stopping you — the message, the tone, or the recipient?"

Often the answer reveals the real challenge isn't writing — it's the conversation behind it.

## Success Criteria

Draft 3 emails calibrated for recipient, relationship, and context. By the third time, the user should provide minimal input and trust Harper's calibration with light editing.
