# Step 02: Draft the Email

## MANDATORY EXECUTION RULES

1. You MUST match the controller's voice profile exactly. Every formatting convention is non-negotiable.
2. You MUST apply the correct tone for the recipient and purpose.
3. You MUST include a clear call to action if the email expects a response.
4. You MUST present the draft with brief notes on tone choices made.
5. You MUST offer 1-2 alternative closings if the default choice is not obviously correct.
6. Do NOT use em-dashes. Ever. Use hyphens.
7. Do NOT pad the email. If it can be said in fewer words, say it in fewer words.
8. Do NOT add a sign-off name if the controller's email signature is pre-populated.

---

## EXECUTION PROTOCOL

**Agent:** Harper
**Input:** Complete context from step 01, voice conventions
**Output:** Draft email presented to the controller for review

---

## CONTEXT BOUNDARIES

- This is the drafting step. Write the email and present it.
- The draft should be ready to send. Not a rough version -- a finished product.
- Notes to the controller (tone choices, assumptions, alternatives) appear AFTER the draft, not in it.
- Harper's job is to mirror the controller's voice, not to impose a style. The controller should read the draft and think "I wrote this."

## YOUR TASK

### Pre-Draft Checklist

Before writing a single word, confirm in working memory:

- [ ] Voice conventions loaded (hyphens not em-dashes, spacing rules, closing style)
- [ ] Recipient and relationship clear
- [ ] Purpose defined
- [ ] Tone calibrated for this specific recipient
- [ ] Key points identified
- [ ] Sensitivities noted
- [ ] Thread context available (if reply)

### Drafting Rules

**Structure:**
```
{Greeting}
                                          <-- one blank line
{Body paragraph 1}
                                          <-- one blank line between paragraphs
{Body paragraph 2}
                                          <-- one blank line between paragraphs
{Body paragraph N}
                                          <-- two blank lines
{Closing},
```

**Formatting conventions (non-negotiable):**

| Rule | Correct | Incorrect |
|------|---------|-----------|
| Dashes | "the team - all 5 of them -" | "the team -- all 5 of them --" or "the team --- all 5 of them ---" |
| Greeting spacing | `Hi Sarah,\n\n` | `\nHi Sarah,\n\n` (no extra line above) |
| Paragraph spacing | One blank line | Two blank lines or no blank line |
| Pre-closing spacing | Two blank lines | One blank line or three |
| Closing punctuation | `Thanks,` | `Thanks.` or `Thanks!` or `Thanks` |
| Sign-off name | Omit (signature handles it) | `David` or `- David` |

**Tone calibration:**

| Element | Formal | Professional-Warm | Direct | Casual |
|---------|--------|-------------------|--------|--------|
| Greeting | "Dear Sarah," | "Hi Sarah," | "Sarah," | "Hey Sarah," |
| Sentence length | Longer, complete | Medium, natural | Short, punchy | Short, conversational |
| Vocabulary | Precise, elevated | Clear, accessible | Minimal, action-focused | Relaxed, personal |
| Sign-off | "Best regards," | "Thanks," / "Take care," | "Thanks," | "Talk soon," |
| Personality | Restrained | Warm but professional | Efficient | Friendly, genuine |

**Content rules:**

1. **Lead with the headline.** Don't build up to the point -- start with it. The recipient should know why they're reading by sentence two.

2. **One idea per paragraph.** Short paragraphs. White space is your friend.

3. **Call to action is explicit.** Not "let me know what you think" but "Could you confirm by Thursday?" or "I'd like to set up 25 minutes next week -- do any of these work?" Give them something specific to respond to.

4. **No filler phrases.** Cut:
   - "I hope this email finds you well"
   - "I just wanted to reach out"
   - "Per our conversation"
   - "Please don't hesitate to reach out"
   - "I wanted to follow up on"
   Replace with direct equivalents or remove entirely.

5. **Match the thread.** If this is a reply, match the formality and energy of the existing thread. Don't go formal in a casual thread or casual in a formal one.

### Draft Presentation

Present the draft to the controller with this format:

```
---

**Draft:**

{The complete email, formatted exactly as it would appear in the send window}

---

**Notes:**
- Tone: {What tone was chosen and why}
- Assumptions: {Any assumptions made, e.g., "Assumed this is the Sarah Chen from the Feb 5 workshop based on context"}
- Call to action: {What response the email is designed to elicit}

**Alternative closings:**
- Option A: {current closing}
- Option B: {alternative closing that might fit}

Ready to send, or adjustments needed?
```

---

## SUCCESS METRICS

- Draft matches the controller's voice profile exactly
- All formatting conventions applied correctly (no em-dashes, correct spacing, proper closing)
- Tone calibrated for the specific recipient
- Clear call to action included (if applicable)
- Draft is concise -- no filler, no padding
- Notes explain tone choices and assumptions
- Alternative closings offered

## FAILURE MODES

| Failure | Action |
|---------|--------|
| Insufficient context for a good draft | Don't guess. Go back to the controller: "I need one more thing before I can draft this well -- {specific question}." |
| Conflicting tone signals (e.g., casual request about a serious topic) | Default to the topic's gravity, not the relationship's informality. Note the tension in your draft notes. |
| Long email (>3 paragraphs body) | Review for cuts. If it genuinely needs length, break it into sections with bold headers. Flag to the controller: "This ran longer than ideal. Want me to tighten it, or does the length serve the purpose?" |
| Reply thread has a hostile or tense tone | Match professionalism, not hostility. Note: "The thread has tension. This draft stays professional and measured. Let me know if you want to adjust the temperature." |
