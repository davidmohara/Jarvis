## What You'll Learn

How to build presentations through Harper — from idea to structured deck with narrative arc, supporting data, and visual direction.

## Before We Start

Complete Talking Points. The user should have an upcoming presentation need or a topic they frequently present on.

## Walkthrough

### Step 1: Define the Presentation (5 min)

Trigger the `harper-deck` skill or say "help me build a deck for [topic]."

Harper asks:
- Audience: Who will see this?
- Purpose: Inform, persuade, decide, or inspire?
- Length: How many slides? How much time?
- Context: Is this a keynote, a pitch, an internal update?

### Step 2: Narrative Arc (7 min)

Harper builds the story structure before touching slides:

1. **Opening hook** — Why should the audience care? (Not an agenda slide.)
2. **Problem/tension** — What's at stake?
3. **Insight/solution** — Your unique perspective or recommendation
4. **Evidence** — Data, stories, proof points
5. **Ask/close** — What do you want them to do next?

**Coaching prompt:** "A presentation is a story, not a document read aloud. If your audience can get the same value by reading the slides themselves, you're not presenting — you're narrating a handout."

### Step 3: Slide Outline (8 min)

Map the narrative to specific slides. Harper generates:
- Slide title (the headline, not a label)
- Key message (one per slide)
- Supporting content (bullet, visual, data)

**Coaching prompt:** "Every slide should answer one question: 'What's the one thing I want the audience to take from this slide?' If you can't answer that, the slide doesn't belong."

Review the outline with the user. Cut ruthlessly.

### Step 4: Build the Deck (7 min)

Harper generates the presentation file. Walk through:
- Title slide
- Content slides with proper structure
- Visual placeholders and suggestions
- Speaker notes for key slides

**Coaching prompt:** "The deck is a tool for your performance, not a replacement for it. Minimal text on screen. Your voice carries the story."

### Step 5: Rehearsal Points (3 min)

Harper flags:
- Slides that need the most practice (complex data, emotional beats)
- Transition moments (where narrative shifts)
- Likely audience questions per section

## Reflection

Ask: "If you had to cut this deck in half, which slides survive? Those are your real presentation."

## Success Criteria

Build one presentation from an idea or outline with structure, narrative arc, and speaker notes.
