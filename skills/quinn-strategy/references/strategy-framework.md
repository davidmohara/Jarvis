## The Kernel — Deep Dive <a id="the-kernel"></a>

### Diagnosis
The diagnosis is NOT a data summary or SWOT analysis. It is an **explanatory judgment** — a framing of the competitive situation that draws attention to certain aspects and away from others. A good diagnosis does three things:

1. **Simplifies** — Reduces overwhelming complexity into a comprehensible narrative
2. **Identifies the critical factors** — Names what actually matters vs. what's noise
3. **Suggests a domain of action** — Points toward where intervention will have impact

Diagnosis is not merely understanding the world — it's understanding the challenge you face. What makes it hard? Push past the surface: "Okay, you want to open business in Australia. So why are you and I talking about it? Just tell somebody to do it. What makes it *hard*?"

Example of bad diagnosis: "We face intense competition and need to differentiate."
Example of good diagnosis: "Our competitors have locked up enterprise distribution, but they've ignored the mid-market segment where buying decisions are made by individual managers, not committees. Our challenge is to build a self-service motion before incumbents notice."

### Guiding Policy
The guiding policy is NOT a vision statement. It is a **constraint on action** — it narrows the range of possible moves. Think of it as a set of guardrails.

A strong guiding policy:
- Creates **advantage** by leveraging something the organization does especially well
- Rules out **classes of action** (not just individual actions)
- Can be used by people throughout the organization to make consistent decisions without constant escalation

Example: "We will only pursue accounts where the buyer has direct budget authority and can sign without committee approval. We will not enter RFP processes."

### Coherent Actions
Coherence means the actions **reinforce each other**. If you remove one action, the others become weaker. This is what distinguishes strategy from a priority list.

Actions that fight each other signal incoherence. "Grow revenue AND increase profit margin" — how? Growth usually requires investment, which compresses margins. If you're pursuing both simultaneously without explaining the mechanism, you have baby talk, not strategy.

Test: List the actions. Draw arrows between the ones that depend on or amplify each other. If no arrows exist, you have a to-do list, not a strategy.

## Bad Strategy — Extended Examples <a id="bad-strategy"></a>

### Why Bad Strategy Proliferates
Bad strategy is not simply the absence of good strategy. It is an active practice with its own logic:

1. **Unwillingness to choose** — Strategy requires hard choices. Organizations that can't say no produce strategies that try to please everyone and achieve nothing. A client once had 17 priorities. Seventeen. That's not a strategy, that's a laundry list of everything you wish would happen.

2. **Template-driven planning** — Fill in Vision, Mission, Values, Goals, Objectives, Strategies, Tactics. This template produces documents that look strategic but are actually categorized wish lists. The template doesn't require a diagnosis.

3. **Leadership as substitute for strategy** — The management shelf is now dominated by "leadership" books about perfecting yourself so rays come out of your head and people follow you. This displaces the hard work of diagnosis and choosing. At some point, someone has to say "we're going to do it this way" — and that's embarrassing in today's corporate culture.

4. **The planning fallacy** — Confusing detailed operational planning with strategic thinking. A 50-page plan with Gantt charts and budgets is not strategy if it doesn't explain what challenge it's solving and why this approach will work.

### Fluff Examples
- "Our fundamental strategy is one of customer-centric intermediation" → Says nothing actionable
- "We will deliver best-in-class solutions leveraging our core competencies" → Buzzword bingo
- "Through operational excellence and innovation, we will be the partner of choice" → Every company says this

### Goals-as-Strategy Examples
- "Our strategy is to be #1 in our market" → That's an aspiration, not an approach
- "We will achieve $100M ARR by 2027" → Financial target, not strategy
- "Our strategy is to grow through acquisition" → Growth mode, not strategy (what will you acquire, why, and how will it create advantage?)
- "The 17 intelligence agencies should work together more effectively" → Restating the problem as the solution. You don't have to be a Russian spy to see they're really saying coordination is broken — but there's no diagnosis of *why*.

## Sources of Power — Extended <a id="sources-of-power"></a>

### The Asymmetry Test
The fundamental question: In a competitive situation, what gives you an edge? If two fighters are equally matched, it's 50/50. For strategy, you need to exploit an asymmetry — something different between you and competitors.

"If you tell me you're going to open a restaurant, my answer is: don't. Invest in the S&P 500. The statistics say new restaurants fail. So what makes you think *you* can succeed? 'I really want to succeed' — not good enough. 'I trained under a successful chef' — now that's interesting. Tell me more. Where's the asymmetry?"

### The Magnifying Glass Metaphor
A magnifying glass focusing sunlight to burn a thread illustrates the complete strategic equation:
1. **Source of power** — the sun (some structural advantage)
2. **Focus** — the lens (concentration of effort on a single point)
3. **Target that can be affected** — a black thread, not white (something achievable, not impervious)

All three must be present. Power without focus dissipates. Focus without power achieves nothing. Both aimed at an unaffectable target wastes everything.

### Leverage — Finding the Pivot Point
Leverage in strategy means applying effort where it will have the most disproportionate effect. Like a physical lever, strategic leverage requires:
- A **pivot point** (the insight about where to focus)
- A **concentration of force** (resources applied to that point)
- An understanding of **natural dynamics** being exploited

Ask: "Where in this situation would a small, concentrated effort produce an outsized result?"

### Proximate Objectives — The Art of the Feasible
A proximate objective is one that is close enough to be accomplished. Ambiguity about what's feasible creates paralysis. By setting objectives that are clearly achievable, you create momentum and learning.

Kennedy's "moon by end of decade" worked because physicists could confirm the physics was possible — they just needed to solve engineering problems. "Cure cancer" — nobody can confirm that's achievable in a given timeframe.

Ask: "Is this objective feasible enough that people can actually figure out what to do next? Or is it so distant that it's essentially a wish?"

### Chain-Link Systems — The Weakest Link
A chain-link system is one where overall performance is limited by the weakest component. Critically:
- You cannot improve a chain-link system by only strengthening the strong links
- Incentive systems that reward individual link performance can actually harm chain-link systems
- The IKEA example: design, sourcing, flat-pack shipping, self-assembly — all interdependent. Copy one element and it produces no advantage.

Ask: "Is your system chain-linked? If so, which link is the bottleneck?"

### Advantage — Deepening vs. Broadening
Competitive advantage can be deepened (widen the moat) or broadened (apply to adjacent markets). The most robust advantages are:
- **Isolating mechanisms** — things that prevent competitors from replicating your position
- **Self-reinforcing** — advantages that grow stronger with use (network effects, data advantages, reputation)

The IBM lesson: Gerstner recognized their source of power was that they were respected and present inside every large corporation on the planet. "Bear hug our customers." But that same customer base became a liability when computing moved to the cloud — big companies with big IT departments were the most hesitant to move. The source of power can become the source of weakness when dynamics shift.

### Dynamics — Riding Waves
Strategy should account for where things are *going*, not just where they are:
- **Technology adoption curves** — early advantage accrues to those who move first
- **Deregulation waves** — temporary chaos that rewards the prepared
- **Shifting costs** — when something expensive becomes cheap (or vice versa)
- **Network effects** — the more users you have, the more useful the product becomes. The dominant competitive game in tech right now.
- **Convergence** — when multiple trends intersect, they create step-function opportunities

## The Crux — Solving the Hardest Part <a id="the-crux"></a>

From rock climbing: the crux is the single hardest move or section of a route. Expert climbers plan their entire ascent around the crux. **If you can't do the crux, don't do the climb.**

In strategy, **the crux is the most critical challenge that must be surmounted for the strategy to work**. It is:
- The thing most people would rather not talk about
- The point where "hoping for the best" is most dangerous
- Often not the most obvious challenge, but the most consequential one

### The Design Insight
Skilled designers — engineers, architects, software developers — experience a moment when they focus on what makes a problem hard and suddenly see a way through. This is the strategic insight.

**I.M. Pei and the Louvre:** The challenge was building an entrance in the courtyard without obscuring the palace. Competing needs — let people in, but don't block the view. The insight: build a transparent building. A glass pyramid. You see through to the Louvre behind it. And make it a pyramid so the top doesn't collect dirt.

**SpaceX:** Why is it so hard to reuse a rocket? Because it burns up on re-entry. Insight: "Why not just turn it around and land it on its rocket?" "Not enough fuel." "Why aren't we carrying more fuel?"

These aren't magic — they come from immersing yourself in the nature of the problem. The insight doesn't always come on command. It might come in the shower, driving, the next day. But it comes from doing the diagnostic work first.

### Finding the Crux
1. List all the challenges in the situation
2. For each: "If we solved everything else but NOT this, would the strategy still work?"
3. The one where the answer is "no" — that's your crux
4. If multiple qualify, the crux is the one that is *hardest to solve*

### Working the Crux
- Concentrate your best thinking and resources on the crux
- If you can't solve the crux, the rest of the strategy is moot — find out fast
- Sometimes identifying the crux reframes the entire strategic challenge

## Strategy as Hypothesis <a id="strategy-as-hypothesis"></a>

Strategy is NOT a plan to be executed. It is a **hypothesis** — an educated guess about what will work.

This framing has important implications:
1. **Strategies should be testable** — What evidence would prove the hypothesis wrong?
2. **Strategies should be updateable** — New information should lead to revision, not denial
3. **The first version is rarely right** — Be approximately right and then learn
4. **Speed of learning matters** — The faster you test, the faster you refine

Ask: "What would have to be true for this to work? What would we expect to see in 90 days? What would tell us we're wrong?"

## Growth Strategy <a id="growth-strategy"></a>

Growth as a strategy deserves deep skepticism:
- **Healthy growth** is the result of superior products, rising demand, or growing competence — not a goal in itself
- **Acquisition-driven growth** often destroys value because buyers overpay and face integration challenges
- **Growth by diversification** rarely works unless the new business benefits from the same sources of power
- **The growth trap**: When growth becomes the objective, organizations stop asking "why will we win?" and start asking "how do we get bigger?"

Ask: "Is growth the *result* of your strategy or the *goal*? If it's the goal, what's the diagnosis?"

## Design Thinking in Strategy <a id="design-strategy"></a>

Strategy can function like a design — an integrated system of choices where each element reinforces the others. Design-type strategies are especially powerful because they're hard to copy piecemeal:

- **Internal consistency** — No element contradicts another
- **Mutual reinforcement** — Elements strengthen each other
- **Fit with the environment** — The design matches the competitive context
- **Inimitability** — Copying one piece doesn't give the competitor the advantage

## The Foundry — Group Strategy Sessions <a id="the-foundry"></a>

When strategy must be built with a team (not solo), use the foundry process. This is designed for the CEO plus 5-8 senior leaders over 2-4 days.

### Why Organizations Struggle
Senior executives typically *know* everything needed to create strategy. They don't need consulting firms to analyze things. But they disagree about the importance of different things. And more importantly, they occupy positions of power — going one direction hurts one person's domain and helps another's. Strategy inside an organization is a problem of **diversity of interest and fear of action**.

Action means changing what people do. It means changing power relationships. Someone who was the alpha may not be anymore. This is heavy stuff. And CEOs are increasingly hesitant to make these calls.

### The Foundry Process
1. **Start with ambitions** — let everyone state theirs. Accept them all. Write them all on the board.
2. **Surface challenges** — for each ambition: "What's holding us back? What makes this hard?" Get 25-50 challenges up on the wall.
3. **The realization** — when people see 25-50 challenges stacked up, they realize: "Oh geez, we better focus. We can't do them all." Focus becomes visceral, not theoretical.
4. **Filter for the overlap** — Which challenges are both *important* (close to ambitions) AND *addressable* (you can actually do something)?
5. **Build action agendas** — for the top 1-2 challenges: what are the coherent actions? Not goals. Things you're going to *do*. Action steps that extend 6 months to 2-3 years.
6. **The decider** — someone must have authority to say "it's going to be this way." Without that hierarchy, you get consensus theater.

### Post-Foundry
People will ask: "Where's the strategy document? Where's the mission, vision, values?" That's not what this produces. This produces an action agenda — and the foundry is designed to make that agenda robust, shared, and executable.

## Value Denials — Finding Innovation Opportunities <a id="value-denials"></a>

The value denials exercise surfaces strategic opportunity by asking: **What should you be able to buy (or do) but can't?**

### Examples
- "I want to buy baggage assurance on my nonstop flight — guaranteed my bag won't be lost." Not available.
- "I want to hire someone to remodel my home on time and on budget." Can't buy it at any price.
- "In Hong Kong, you can drop luggage off downtown and it arrives at the airport checked in. Why not in the US?"

### The Perfect Window Exercise
Take any familiar object and ask: "What would the perfect version look like?"
- A window should let in light... but sometimes you want darkness (drapes/shades)
- It should let in air... but not bugs (screen)
- It should let in air... but not noise (acoustic filters — someone at MIT built this)

Every gap between the perfect version and the current reality is a potential innovation. Now apply this to your business, your industry, your customers' experience.

### Where to Look
- Places where regulation blocks innovation (plumbing codes, dealer laws, union rules)
- Places where "how it's always been done" persists despite better alternatives
- Places where customers are resigned to bad experiences

## Strategy for Startups and New Ventures <a id="startup-strategy"></a>

Startups face maximum uncertainty. Strategy here is different from corporate strategy:

### The Bet
You're making a bet: "There's a set of customers who want X, are being denied X, and we have a way of giving them X." The bet has three components: target market, solution, and your right to win.

### The Search
Research on Silicon Valley startups shows the initial bet usually doesn't work. The ones that survive and prosper **switch** — different customer, slightly different product, different market. It's a search, like a truffle hound searching for the truffle, over 1-2 years, adjusting until something clicks.

### The Double-Jointed Mind
You must hold two contradictory things simultaneously:
1. **Be convinced** you're going to win — commit fully
2. **Be willing to shift** when things aren't working — adapt fast

This is a human skill some people can do and some can't. Both commitment AND flexibility.

### The Action Agenda for Startups
- Be clear about the nature of your bet
- Know that reality will reveal itself in bits and pieces
- Make the action agenda about **quick adaptation to changing conditions**
- Time horizon: 6 months, not 5 years

## Coaching Questions Bank <a id="coaching-questions"></a>

### For Diagnosis
- "Forget about solutions for a moment — what is the fundamental challenge here?"
- "If you had to explain what's going on to someone who knows nothing about your industry, in two sentences, what would you say?"
- "What has changed recently that makes this challenge more urgent?"
- "What would a brutally honest outsider say the real problem is?"
- "Is this a problem of not knowing what to do, or not being able to do what you know?"
- "Okay, so why are you and I talking about it? What makes it *hard*?"
- "That's not a diagnosis, that's a statement of dissatisfaction. Why is it hard to fix?"

### For Guiding Policy
- "What are you saying NO to with this approach?"
- "If a competitor knew your guiding policy, could they easily counter it?"
- "Does this policy leverage something you do better than anyone else?"
- "Can someone three levels down in your organization use this policy to make a decision?"
- "What would violate this guiding policy? Give me an example of something you'd turn down."

### For Coherent Actions
- "If you did these three things but they weren't coordinated, would it still work?"
- "Which of these actions is the one you're most tempted to skip? That might be the most important one."
- "Can you actually do all of these with your current resources and capabilities?"
- "What happens first? What depends on what?"
- "If you could only do ONE of these actions, which would it be? Why?"
- "Are these things you're going to *do*, or goals you wish would happen?"

### For Stress-Testing
- "What assumption, if wrong, would make this strategy completely fail?"
- "What would your smartest competitor do in response to this strategy?"
- "Is this strategy different enough from what everyone else is doing to actually matter?"
- "In 12 months, what's the most likely reason this didn't work?"
- "Where is the strategy relying on hope rather than evidence?"

### For The Crux
- "What's the hardest part of this challenge — the thing you'd rather not think about?"
- "If you solve everything except X, does the strategy still work?"
- "What's the one question you're avoiding?"
- "Is there a capability gap that no amount of motivation can bridge?"

### For Sources of Power
- "Where is the asymmetry? What do you have that competitors don't?"
- "If this were a restaurant, would I tell you to invest in the S&P instead? What makes your odds better than average?"
- "What have you mastered, or own, or inherited from the past that others can't easily replicate?"
- "Is there a way to redefine your space small enough that you can see your advantage?"

## Common Traps and How to Escape Them <a id="common-traps"></a>

### The Consensus Trap
**Symptom:** Strategy is so broad and vague that no one disagrees with it.
**Escape:** Demand specificity. A strategy everyone agrees with probably doesn't exclude enough.

### The Spreadsheet Trap
**Symptom:** Strategy is defined as hitting financial targets. "We need 20% growth" becomes "each division will grow 20%." This is just distributing the goal, not creating a strategy.
**Escape:** Go back to diagnosis. Why will each division grow? What's the mechanism?

### The Innovation Theater Trap
**Symptom:** "Our strategy is innovation." Innovation labs, hackathons, and transformation offices everywhere, but no clear diagnosis.
**Escape:** Ask what specific problem the innovation is solving and why you're better positioned to solve it.

### The Me-Too Trap
**Symptom:** Strategy is essentially "do what the market leader does, but try harder."
**Escape:** Find the asymmetry. If nowhere, you need a fundamentally different approach.

### The Hedging Trap
**Symptom:** Strategy pursues multiple contradictory paths "just in case."
**Escape:** Strategy is choosing. Hedging is the opposite. Name the bet.

### The Leadership-as-Strategy Trap
**Symptom:** "If we just had better leadership, we'd succeed." Leadership development substitutes for strategic thinking. Everyone reads leadership books; nobody does diagnosis.
**Escape:** Leadership is necessary but not sufficient. You still need someone to say what you're actually going to *do* differently. "Perfecting yourself" doesn't produce an action agenda.

### The Matrix Organization Trap
**Symptom:** Nokia-style diffusion — matrix organization so complex that nobody is actually in charge of anything. The CEO pounds the desk saying "somebody make a touchscreen phone" but there's no one with the authority to actually do it.
**Escape:** Simplify authority. If you want strategic action, someone must own the decision and the execution. Matrix structures are designed for coordination, not for bold strategic moves.

## Vivid Examples for Teaching <a id="vivid-examples"></a>

### The Magnifying Glass (Focus + Power + Target)
A boy at summer camp with a magnifying glass trying to burn cloth. Can't do it. Counselor pulls out a black thread. Focus the sun on the black thread — pop. Source of power (the sun), focus (the lens), a target that can actually be affected (black thread, not white). All three must be present.

### The Restaurant Test (Asymmetry)
"I'm going to open a restaurant." "Don't. Invest in the S&P 500." Statistics say restaurants fail. Where's your asymmetry? "I trained under a successful chef" — now we're talking. But "I really want to succeed" is not a source of power.

### IBM's Bear Hug (Power from Relationships, Then Liability)
Gerstner recognized IBM's primary asset was being respected and present inside every large corporation. "Bear hug our customers." But when computing moved to the cloud, those same large customers were the most hesitant to change — IBM's power became its constraint.

### The I.M. Pei Pyramid (Crux as Design Insight)
Build an entrance in the Louvre courtyard without blocking the palace view. Competing needs. Insight: build it out of glass — transparent, you see through to the Louvre. Make it a pyramid so the top stays clean. The crux forced the insight.

### SpaceX (Crux as Engineering Challenge)
Why is reusing a rocket hard? Burns up on re-entry. "Turn it around and land on its rocket." "Not enough fuel." "Carry more fuel." The crux (re-entry destruction) forced a rethinking of the entire rocket design.

### The 17 Intelligence Agencies (Goals as Strategy)
US strategy for 17 intelligence agencies: "They should work together more effectively." That's restating the problem as the solution. No diagnosis of *why* they don't coordinate. No action agenda. This has been going on for 34 years.

### Nokia's Collapse (Matrix + No Decider)
Nokia replaced engineers with lawyers and accountants. Put in a matrix organization that diffused power. The CEO kept saying "Apple has a touchscreen phone, somebody here make one." But nobody had the authority to actually do it. Result: strategic paralysis.
